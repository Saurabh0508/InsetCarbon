SBTi FLAG GUIDANCE

Why FLAG Matters for Agricultural Value Chains

The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) Forest, Land and Agriculture (FLAG) Guidance provides a standardized framework for companies with land-based value chains to set science-based emissions reduction targets. For companies sourcing agricultural commodities, FLAG represents the most credible pathway to address the substantial emissions footprint embedded within supply chains.

Under FLAG, companies must account for emissions and removals across forest, land, and agriculture systems. This includes a requirement that at least 20% of near-term FLAG targets be addressed through active carbon removal and land-based interventions within value chains. Enhanced Rock Weathering (ERW) offers a verified, measurable pathway to meet these removal obligations.

FLAG REQUIREMENTS AT A GLANCE
Measure FLAG Emissions
Companies must account for land-use change, land management, and soil carbon impacts across agricultural supply chains. FLAG covers all emissions linked to forest, land, and agriculture activities within Scope 3.
Reduce and Remove
FLAG requires a minimum 72% reduction in land-use emissions by 2050, alongside active carbon removal interventions. At least 20% of near-term targets must come from within-value-chain removals.
Demonstrate Progress
Companies must report FLAG-related removals transparently, with independent verification. ERW credits issued under ISOMETRIC or Puro.earth protocols qualify as FLAG-aligned carbon removals.

Connection to ERW Insetting. Enhanced Rock Weathering directly supports FLAG compliance by generating permanent carbon removals within agricultural value chains. ERW deployment on cropland sequesters atmospheric CO₂ as stable bicarbonate for over 10,000 years, improves soil health, and delivers measurable yield benefits to farmers. Each tonne of CO₂ removed is independently verified and registered on a public ledger, providing the audit trail required for FLAG reporting.

For companies with extensive agricultural sourcing footprints, ERW insetting transforms carbon liabilities into verified removals, strengthens supplier relationships through direct farmer engagement, and delivers soil and yield co-benefits that enhance supply chain resilience.

Block-level GEE screen on priority districts: basalt within 120 km AND topsoil pH ≤ 6.7 AND dominant-crop LULC (MNCFC kharif raster). Result: 27,000–44,000 ha across screened PepsiCo sourcing districts with confirmed basalt access and suitable soil pH.

More than carbon. soil, yield, savings, permanence

METHODOLOGY

Basalt is a silicate base; as it weathers, it releases Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ that buffer soil acidity. Field-trial data from Beerling et al. (Nature, 2020) on UK acidic croplands shows +0.2 pH in season 1, +0.4–0.6 by season 3. Our range conservatively assumes Indian croplands respond in line with subtropical field conditions.

METHODOLOGY

Yield response is driven by (1) pH correction, (2) Ca/Mg release, and (3) silicon availability. Trials at Cornell (Reinhardt 2024) and ETH Zürich (Vienne 2022) report 5–15% yield uplift on pH-corrected acidic soils; we use the conservative end for first-pilot pricing.

METHODOLOGY

ERW permanence comes from converting CO₂ to stable bicarbonate that is transported to the ocean, and is geologically stable for >10,000 years. Forestry credits face reversal risk from fires, pests, and harvest, with effective permanence widely modelled at 20–100 years. Permanence multiple: ~100×.

METHODOLOGY

Basalt substitutes for agricultural lime (₹2,500–4,000/ha) and reduces N-P-K demand by 8–15% via enhanced cation exchange (₹1,500–3,000/ha). Micronutrient (Mg, Si, Fe) input savings add ~₹500/ha. Year-1 net savings typically ₹4,000–8,000 per hectare; widens further as soil structure improves.
